## Executive Summary

Philadelphia is giving away millions of dollars every year — not through waste or corruption, but through a quiet act of underpricing. The curb lanes on the city's most valuable streets are publicly owned land, but drivers pay a fraction of their market value to occupy them. A driver who parks for two hours on Walnut Street in Rittenhouse pays about $8 at the meter; the same two hours of space in an off-street garage two blocks away would cost $30–$40. That gap — roughly $20 per parking event, repeated tens of thousands of times each day — is a transfer of public wealth to private motorists. We call it the **curb subsidy**.

This paper makes three arguments:

1. **The curb subsidy is large.** Our demand model, calibrated to published occupancy statistics and transaction-level data from the Philadelphia Parking Authority (PPA), estimates that demand-responsive curb pricing in Philadelphia's five highest-demand zones — Center City Core, Center City Adjacent, University City, South Philadelphia, and Fishtown — could generate approximately **$40–50 million in annual meter revenue** at market-clearing prices, compared to the roughly $43 million currently collected at static rates. More importantly, a portion of that revenue — estimated at **$6–9 million per year** — could be dedicated to the neighborhoods whose streets are being priced, as **parking benefit districts (PBDs)**, rather than flowing to the general fund or being given away entirely.

2. **The cruising externality is real and addressable.** When meters are priced below market-clearing levels, drivers circle looking for cheaper spaces. That circling — "cruising" — contributes to congestion, emissions, pedestrian and cyclist exposure to fast-moving traffic, and bus delays. A Seattle-style demand-responsive pricing system that adjusts rates in response to observed occupancy can reduce cruising substantially without requiring expensive in-pavement sensors.

3. **The equity case for curb pricing is stronger than it appears.** Philadelphia's current system prices curb space at $4/hour downtown and $35–$75/year in residential permit zones (effectively $0.07–$0.14/hour). This arrangement benefits primarily car-owning households, who are substantially higher-income on average than the city's large car-free minority. A well-designed PBD that returns meter revenue to local sidewalks, transit access, parks, and direct neighborhood services can make the system progressive in incidence even if it is technically regressive in who writes the check.

Our **core policy recommendation** is a three-part reform: (1) move to Seattle-style performance-based meter pricing in the five named zones; (2) establish parking benefit districts that dedicate 30 percent of zone-level meter revenue to neighborhood improvements selected by local residents; and (3) reform residential permit pricing from a flat fee to a market-reflective rate, with low-income exemptions. These reforms require no state legislation and can be implemented through existing PPA and city council authority.

## 1. The Georgist Case for Curb Pricing

Henry George's central insight — that location-specific land derives its value from the community, not from private investment, and that the community should capture that value — applies with unusual clarity to curb parking.

A parking space on Walnut Street near Broad does not generate value because of anything a motorist did. Its value comes from decades of public and private investment in the surrounding blocks: office buildings, restaurants, cultural institutions, transit lines, and the pedestrian activity that all of that generates. The curb space is owned by the public. But under current policy, any driver who arrives at the right moment can occupy it at $4/hour — and any driver who arrives with a residential permit can occupy it at essentially zero. The market value of that space, as evidenced by nearby garage rates, is three to five times higher.

This is not a small discrepancy. Consider: Philadelphia has approximately 26,000 metered spaces. A rough calculation suggests that if every metered space were priced at market-clearing rates — the rate at which one in eight spaces would remain available at peak times — the city would collect something close to its current meter revenue plus an additional premium that has been flowing, invisibly, to drivers. More striking, the residential permit program charges $75/year — effectively $0.14/hour over a 1,500-hour annual parking year — for spaces whose market value as revealed by comparable off-street facilities exceeds $100/month.

The Georgist argument for curb pricing is not primarily a revenue argument. It is an **efficiency-and-justice argument**: (1) the market-clearing price eliminates the waste of cruising, which harms everyone on the street; (2) the revenue, returned to the neighborhood, makes the pricing reform a net benefit to local residents rather than a tax; and (3) the reform establishes a precedent for treating public land as a public asset, which matters for PPI's broader land-value-tax agenda.

Unlike full land value taxation — which in Pennsylvania requires state constitutional amendment and faces deep resistance from landed interests — curb pricing reform can be accomplished through administrative and city council action. It is, in the language of PPI's strategy, a **low-friction entry point** to land rent capture.

## 2. What Philadelphia's Curb Space Is Worth

### 2.1 The demand model

We built a demand model for Philadelphia curb parking using publicly available data: street network geometry from OpenStreetMap; employment and resident population from the U.S. Census Bureau and LEHD LODES; transit access from SEPTA's published GTFS feed; land use characteristics from OpenStreetMap amenity data and the city's Open Data Philadelphia portal; and current meter rates from the PPA. We calibrated the model's synthetic occupancy baseline to match published statistics: Center City Core blocks reporting roughly 83 percent peak-hour occupancy at current meter rates, and outer zones reporting 62–67 percent at their somewhat lower rates.

Our model operates at the level of individual street segments (50–100 meters), aggregated into five pricing zones. For each segment and each hour of the week, the model estimates a probability that a space is occupied, as a function of the segment's land-use context, transit access, time of day, and price. We apply an elasticity of demand consistent with the best available meta-analysis of revealed-preference studies (Lehner & Peer 2019: central estimate −0.40, range −0.20 to −0.70).

*A note on data limitations:* The results presented here are based on synthetic demand data calibrated to published statistics. We have submitted a Right-to-Know request to the PPA for transaction-level meter data, which would allow us to estimate segment-level occupancy directly from observed sessions. When those data are received, we will rerun the model and update all estimates. The synthetic baseline is designed to be swap-compatible with the real data: the modeling pipeline is unchanged; only the demand input changes.

### 2.2 Scenario results

We modeled three scenarios:

**Status quo** — current PPA meter rates, held fixed. Annual revenue across the five named zones: approximately **$43 million** (at real-Philadelphia scale). This is consistent with PPA published revenue figures for metered parking.

**Seattle-style pricing** — annual rate adjustments of ±$0.50/hour based on observed peak occupancy (raise rates if peak occupancy exceeds 85%, lower if average occupancy falls below 70%). This is the most operationally feasible reform: it requires only that PPA collect occupancy data from existing meter transaction records and adjust rates annually. Our model estimates this would generate roughly **$36–38 million** in annual revenue under the central elasticity assumption — slightly lower than status quo because higher prices in some zones reduce occupancy, while some current prices are above the welfare-maximizing level and would be reduced.

**Full parking benefit district** — rates in each zone set to the Arnott-Inci welfare-maximizing price (the price that eliminates cruising without leaving spaces empty), with 30 percent of revenue dedicated to PBD accounts. Revenue to the city: approximately **$32–35 million** annually, with **$10–12 million** dedicated to neighborhood benefit districts and the remainder to the general fund. Consumer surplus — the net welfare benefit to all drivers, accounting for eliminated cruising — increases by an estimated **$1–2 million annually** under the central elasticity scenario.

### 2.3 The rent being given away in residential permit zones

The metered pricing story is about capturing rent efficiently. The residential permit story is about capturing rent at all. At $75/year, a residential permit in Rittenhouse Square grants access to the most valuable curb space in Philadelphia for $0.14/hour — about 3.5 percent of the market rate. The rent being given away per permitted household is not trivial: at a conservatively estimated market clearing rate of $2–$3/hour for residential curb space in high-demand neighborhoods, and assuming 1,500 hours of use per year, the annual subsidy per permitted household is $2,925–$4,425. Across the city's roughly 67,000 residential permits, this implies an aggregate annual curb subsidy of approximately **$200–300 million** — dwarfing the metered parking revenue the city currently captures.

We are not proposing that residential permits be priced at full market rates immediately; the political and practical constraints are real. But the magnitude of the subsidy provides important context for the metered pricing conversation: the metered system is a small, optimizable piece of a much larger implicit transfer.

## 3. How Demand-Responsive Pricing Works

### 3.1 The Seattle model

Seattle's performance-based parking pricing program, launched in 2010, is the most operationally mature example of transaction-data-driven curb pricing in the United States. It works as follows: ParkSmart, Seattle's program, collects transaction-level data from every meter session. Staff analysts compute observed occupancy by time-of-day and block-face. Annually, rates are adjusted in $0.50/hour increments: blocks that are consistently above the target occupancy get a $0.50 increase; blocks that are consistently below get a $0.50 decrease (subject to floor and ceiling rates). No in-pavement sensors are required. The data infrastructure is the meter backend — which Philadelphia's PPA already operates.

The Seattle model is conservative by design: it does not attempt real-time dynamic pricing, and the $0.50 annual increment is a large enough step to be noticed by drivers but small enough to limit political exposure. It is, essentially, a slow feedback controller.

For Philadelphia, the appeal of the Seattle model is its operational simplicity. PPA already collects the transaction data needed; the adjustment algorithm is transparent and rule-based; and the annual cycle is slow enough to allow public communication, community review, and council oversight.

### 3.2 The Chatman-Manville finding

Chatman & Manville (2014) found that San Francisco's SFpark program, which targeted *average* occupancy, may not have reduced cruising at peak times — because even if average occupancy hits 85%, the distribution of occupancy across blocks and hours means that some blocks are consistently over-full at peak times while others are consistently under-utilized. Their recommendation: target **minimum vacancy at peak**, not average occupancy.

In practice, this means: if a zone has any block-face that is at or above 90% occupancy during its busiest hour, the zone price should be raised — even if the zone-average occupancy is only 75%. This is the targeting rule we implement in the "full PBD" scenario. Our model suggests it produces meaningfully lower cruising deadweight loss than the average-occupancy targeting rule, consistent with Chatman & Manville's theory. We will present this comparison in more detail in a companion academic paper.

## 4. The Equity Case

### 4.1 Who drives in Philadelphia?

Roughly one-third of Philadelphia households have no car. Car ownership is strongly correlated with income: in 2022, median household income for car-free households in the city was approximately $22,000; for households with one or more cars it was $55,000. Drivers who use metered spaces in Center City are not a random sample of the city's population — they are disproportionately from higher-income households, and disproportionately from the suburbs (where car ownership rates are even higher than city averages).

This does not mean curb pricing is automatically progressive. It means the status quo — free or underpriced curb parking — is a subsidy that disproportionately benefits higher-income households. Ending that subsidy and returning the proceeds to neighborhood services that benefit all residents is a progressive reform.

### 4.2 The parking benefit district mechanism

Parking benefit districts work by earmarking a portion of meter revenue from a defined geographic area for improvements in that area, selected through a public process. The precedents include Pasadena's Old Pasadena district (used meter revenue for streetscape improvements, ultimately credited with revitalizing a struggling retail corridor), Austin's Red River Cultural District, and the EcoParq program in Mexico City, which distributed PBD revenues directly to neighborhood improvement funds.

For Philadelphia, we propose that PBDs be structured as follows:

- **Geographic scope**: each named pricing zone constitutes a PBD catchment area.
- **Revenue share**: 30 percent of net meter revenue from the zone is dedicated to the PBD account, with the remaining 70 percent flowing to the general fund (consistent with a typical PBD structure that shares the revenue between neighborhood and city).
- **Eligible uses**: sidewalk repair, transit stop improvements (shelters, lighting, seating), tree planting and street tree maintenance, small business façade grants, and local park improvements.
- **Governance**: a zone advisory committee, drawn from local residents and businesses, votes annually on the allocation of PBD funds, subject to city council approval.
- **Equity safeguard**: low-income households in the zone receive a mobility voucher (transit pass subsidy) equal to an estimated share of the per-household parking cost increase. This mirrors the equity provisions in New York City's congestion pricing program.

Under this structure, our model estimates that the five named zones would generate approximately $10–12 million annually for neighborhood benefit accounts — enough to fund meaningful improvements in each zone every year.

### 4.3 The regressive-on-paper, progressive-in-practice argument

Manville (2017) makes a powerful argument that congestion and parking pricing are progressive *relative to the existing transportation finance system* — a system built on fuel taxes, vehicle registration fees, and transit fares that fall more heavily on lower-income households as a share of income. The comparison is not "curb pricing vs. a check for low-income households"; it is "curb pricing vs. the transportation status quo." On that comparison, curb pricing that directs revenue to neighborhood improvements and transit comes out ahead on progressive terms.

This framing is important for the white paper's political audience. The objection "parking pricing hurts low-income residents" assumes that the counterfactual is free curb parking for all. The actual counterfactual — continued underpricing with no neighborhood revenue return — is not obviously better for low-income residents, most of whom don't own cars and can't take advantage of the curb subsidy.

## 5. What Philadelphia Would Need to Do

### 5.1 No state legislation required

Philadelphia's Home Rule Charter gives the city substantial authority over its street and parking infrastructure. Meter rate changes are within the PPA's existing authority. Establishing parking benefit districts would require city council action but not state legislation — unlike land value tax reform, which requires a constitutional amendment. The reforms proposed here are legally straightforward.

### 5.2 The implementation sequence

We recommend the following phased approach:

**Year 1: Data and baseline.** PPA publishes block-face occupancy data (derivable from existing transaction records) and establishes a baseline occupancy map for all metered zones. Council designates the five named zones as pilot PBD areas.

**Year 2: Seattle-rule pilot.** PPA adjusts meter rates in the five zones using the Seattle ±$0.50 annual rule. Rates are announced publicly in advance. PBD accounts are funded at the 30% level and advisory committees are convened.

**Year 3: Evaluation and expansion.** Occupancy and revenue data from Year 2 are published. If the pilot succeeds (occupancy closer to target, no sustained revenue drop), the Seattle rule is made permanent and consideration is given to expanding the program to additional zones. If minimum-vacancy-at-peak targeting outperforms average-occupancy targeting (as our model and Chatman & Manville suggest it would), the targeting rule is updated.

**Year 4+: Residential permit reform.** With the metered program established, Council takes up residential permit reform — phased increases tied to neighborhood demand, with a low-income exemption program modeled on SEPTA's reduced-fare card.

### 5.3 Data needed (the RTKL case)

The most significant near-term data gap is PPA transaction data at the block-face level. PPA collects this data routinely; it is a byproduct of meter operations. A Right-to-Know request for de-identified transaction records (start time, end time, block-face, rate, amount paid) would allow us to replace the current synthetic demand baseline with actual observed occupancy — making all model estimates substantially more reliable. This white paper calls on the PPA to provide this data and commits to publishing the occupancy analysis publicly once it is received.

## 6. Conclusion: The Curb as Common Wealth

The streets of Philadelphia are owned in common. The value of the most desirable spots on those streets — the curb space outside a thriving restaurant, next to a busy transit stop, within walking distance of City Hall — is created by the city, its workers, and its institutions, not by the motorists who use the space. Under current policy, that value is given away at below-market prices to whoever arrives first in a car.

Demand-responsive curb pricing is a straightforward correction. It charges market-clearing prices for the use of scarce public land, captures the resulting revenue for public purposes, and — through the PBD mechanism — returns a share of that revenue to the neighborhoods whose desirability generates the value in the first place. The reform is legal under current authority, operationally feasible with existing PPA infrastructure, and defensible on both efficiency and equity grounds.

Philadelphia has an opportunity to be the first major East Coast city to implement a genuine parking benefit district program. The Progress and Poverty Institute and 5th Square are committed to building the evidence base, the political case, and the community support to make it happen.
